4.1 Why HIPS for the Armor

HIPS is amorphous — it neither shrinks anisotropically on cooling nor sets up internal crystalline stresses. This allows molded armor pieces to faithfully reproduce sub-millimeter panel lines. Its Tg ≈ 95–100 °C sits comfortably above all handling temperatures. Its dispersed polybutadiene phase (1–3 μm) raises impact toughness 10× over general-purpose polystyrene without sacrificing modulus by more than ≈20%.

4.2 Why LLDPE for the Polycap

Three properties of LLDPE make it ideal for polycap joints. First, its low modulus (≈0.2–0.5 GPa) gives the socket enough compliance to accommodate the diametral interference without cracking. Second, its semicrystalline morphology means it relaxes only slowly under sustained load — polycap grip force is maintained across hundreds of pose changes, bounded by crystalline domains acting as physical crosslinks. Third, polyethylene's low surface energy (≈31 mJ/m²) means the joint slides cleanly against the HIPS peg without stick-slip.

4.3 Why Polycaps Are Not Glued

Polyethylene is non-polar and notoriously difficult to bond. Most cyanoacrylate, model-cement, and epoxy adhesives require a wetting surface energy above ≈35 mJ/m² to develop interfacial adhesion. Without surface treatment (corona, flame, or chemical etch), neither cyanoacrylate nor polystyrene-cement will bond polyethylene. Bandai's design therefore uses interference fit and snap geometry rather than chemical bonding — a constraint set entirely by the polymer chemistry.
