4. Material Properties of the Plastics

4.1 Why I Looked at This

How a GunPla kit snaps together, holds a pose, and why some joints go floppy after years of repositioning — all of this is explained by polymer mechanical properties. Put numbers on stiffness, impact toughness, glass transition, and surface energy for styrenic hard runners and LLDPE polycaps, and the design choices in the kit start to look inevitable.

The Board A scan does not create a whole new property regime: SB-13 and Board A are both styrenic hard-runner formulations, while PC7 is polyethylene. The largest property split is therefore between the rigid styrenic family and the polycap material. 4.2 Why HIPS for the Armor

HIPS is amorphous — it neither shrinks anisotropically on cooling nor sets up internal crystalline stresses. This allows molded armor pieces to faithfully reproduce sub-millimeter panel lines. Its glass transition Tg ≈ 95–100 °C sits comfortably above all handling temperatures, so the kit does not creep at room temperature or in a sun-warmed display case. Its dispersed polybutadiene phase (1–3 μm) raises notched impact toughness by roughly an order of magnitude over general-purpose polystyrene without sacrificing modulus by more than ≈20%.

4.3 Why LLDPE for the Polycap

LLDPE works for polycap joints because of three properties. Its low modulus (≈0.2–0.5 GPa) gives the socket enough compliance to accommodate diametral interference without cracking. Its semicrystalline morphology relaxes only slowly under sustained load — polycap grip force is maintained across hundreds of pose changes, bounded by crystalline domains acting as physical crosslinks. Polyethylene's low surface energy (≈31 mJ/m²) means the joint slides cleanly against the HIPS peg without stick-slip.

4.4 Why Polycaps Are Not Glued

Polyethylene is non-polar and notoriously difficult to bond. Most cyanoacrylate, model-cement, and epoxy adhesives require a wetting surface energy above ≈35 mJ/m² to develop interfacial adhesion. Without surface treatment (corona, flame, or chemical etch), neither cyanoacrylate nor polystyrene-cement will bond polyethylene. Bandai's design therefore uses interference fit and snap geometry rather than chemical bonding — a constraint set entirely by the polymer chemistry.

Design Consequence

Choosing polyethylene for the polycap forecloses adhesives as a joining strategy. Every articulation point on the kit therefore has to be solved with mechanical interference geometry instead. The constraint is upstream of the design — the polymer choice locks in the assembly method.
