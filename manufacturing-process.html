Manufacturing Process

How Gundam model kits are produced at scale

3. Injection Molding Production Process

Bandai's production of Gunpla at the Bandai Hobby Center in Shizuoka relies on two technologies that distinguish modern Gunpla from generic plastic models: (i) System Injection multi-color molding and (ii) tightly toleranced mold cavities that allow finished parts to engage one another by snap-fit alone. Multi-color molds inject up to four different polymer colors into a single mold tool by sequentially opening and closing gate valves between successive injection cycles. The result is a single runner that emerges from the press already containing more than one color of plastic — the basis for the "no paint required" promise of an HG kit.

Why It Matters

Multi-color runners reduce packaging volume and eliminate post-processing painting, making the "no paint required" HG promise possible at scale.

3.1 Process Steps

1
Pellet Drying
HIPS and polyethylene pellets are dried under hot air (70–80 °C for HIPS, 60–70 °C for PE) to remove moisture below ≈0.05 wt.% to prevent surface defects and hydrolytic discoloration.
2
Plasticization
A reciprocating screw extruder melts pellets at ≈200–240 °C for HIPS and ≈180–240 °C for LLDPE, building melt pressure ahead of the screw tip.
3
Injection
The screw advances axially as a piston, driving the melt through a hot runner system into the cooled mold cavity at injection pressures typically of 50–150 MPa.
4
Multi-Color Sequencing
In Bandai's System Injection process, the mold contains gates for up to four colors. After the first color is injected, its gates close, the next color's gates open, and a different barrel injects the next polymer — cycling until the runner is complete.
5
Cooling and Ejection
Cooling channels maintain tool temperature at ≈30–60 °C; cycle times for HG-class runners are on the order of 30–60 s. Ejector pins push the solidified runner out and a take-out robot moves it to the inspection conveyor.
6
Packaging
Inspected runners are sealed in polyethylene bags with the instruction manual and decals, and the kit is boxed for shipment.

3.2 Why Multi-Color Molding Is Non-Trivial

Bandai's multi-color process is engineering-noteworthy because the same mold cavity must hold molten polymer at very different bulk viscosities without bleeding color. HIPS at 220 °C has a melt viscosity roughly an order of magnitude higher than LLDPE at the same temperature, which means the gate diameters and runner geometries on a multi-color mold are tuned per polymer rather than uniformly.
